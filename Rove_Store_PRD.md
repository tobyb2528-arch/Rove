## 1. Overview

### 1.1 Purpose
Define the requirements for Rove's core store pages. The **product page** is the single most important conversion surface and is spec'd in most detail; the **FAQ, Contact, and Cart** pages are supporting surfaces that reinforce trust and remove friction across the buyer journey. Together they must make a dropshipped cooler backpack feel like a premium, trustworthy, own-brand product, justifying a £39–42 price point against near-identical unbranded listings selling for £15–20.

### 1.2 Background
Rove is a new outdoor/adventure gear brand. The launch product is an insulated 30-can backpack cooler. Hundreds of competing stores sell the identical product using the same supplier photos and keyword-stuffed copy. Rove's entire competitive edge is **presentation**: a distinct brand, clean design, honest trust-building, and consistent imagery.

Two reference sites define the target quality bar:
- **Season Three** — the benchmark for *visual design and feel*: editorial minimalism, named products, benefit-led copy, clean single-CTA layout.
- **Huru** — the benchmark for *trust and functionality*: a spelled-out guarantee, transparent shipping, specs framed as benefits, prominent reviews.

The Rove page = Season Three's clean editorial front-end + Huru's trust-and-detail engine underneath.

### 1.3 Target customer
Primary ICP: **The Weekend Adventurer** (27–40, values experiences, mid price-sensitivity, mobile/social-led, wants gear that looks good and won't let her down). Full ICP documented separately.

## 2. Goals & success metrics

### 2.1 Goals
- Present the cooler as a premium own-brand product, not a generic reseller listing.
- Remove purchase doubt (leaking, cheapness, "is this just AliExpress?").
- Sell the *feeling* (cold drinks, hands-free, day-out freedom), backed by honest detail.
- Convert cold social traffic (TikTok/Instagram) on mobile.

### 2.2 Success metrics
| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Product-page conversion rate | ≥ 2.5% (good for cold social traffic) |
| Add-to-cart rate | ≥ 8% |
| Mobile bounce rate | < 55% |
| Avg. price point held | £39–42 (no discounting to compete on price) |
| Return rate | < 5% (honest copy reduces "not as described") |

### 2.3 Non-goals
- Not building a large multi-category catalogue at launch — one hero product, done excellently.
- No blog, no complex animations, no loyalty program at launch.
- No trust claims the business cannot honour (see §6).

---

## 3. Design principles

1. **Editorial minimalism (Season Three).** Big imagery, generous white space, let the product breathe. Clutter reads as cheap.
2. **Sell the feeling first, specs second.** Emotional benefit up top; technical detail available but tucked away.
3. **One clear path.** A single primary CTA. No competing buttons above the fold.
4. **Trust is a feature (Huru).** Guarantee, shipping, and reviews are core page elements, not afterthoughts.
5. **Mobile-first.** Design for a phone screen first; desktop is the enhancement.
6. **Honest by default.** Every claim is true to what a dropshipped product can deliver.

## 4. Product page — structure & requirements

*The product page is the primary conversion surface and the most detailed section of this PRD.*

### 4.1 Header / navigation
- Minimal top nav: logo (Rove), Shop, About, Cart.
- Persistent free-shipping / trust strip (e.g. "Free UK delivery over £40 · 30-day returns").
- **Priority:** Must-have.

### 4.2 Product hero (above the fold)
Modelled on Season Three.
- **Large lifestyle product image** — cooler in a real day-out moment (beach/trail/campsite), not a plain white-background cutout. Image gallery/thumbnails for additional angles.
- **Named product:** "The Rove Cooler Pack" (plus colour name, e.g. "Slate Green").
- **Price**, clearly displayed in GBP.
- **Benefit one-liner** directly under price: e.g. *"An all-day insulated cooler you wear on your back — cold drinks, anywhere the day takes you."*
- **Variant selectors:** colour swatches + quantity.
- **Single primary CTA:** "Add to Cart" (on-brand alt wording optional, but keep it one clear button).
- **One bold, honest tip** (Season Three's half-size-tip equivalent): e.g. *"Pro tip: freeze two water bottles overnight — they double as ice packs and drinks."*
- **Priority:** Must-have.

### 4.3 Benefit / "Why Rove" block
Modelled on Huru's feature framing.
- 3–4 icon + short-benefit blocks: **All-Day Cold · Leakproof Liner · Hands-Free Carry · Built to Last.**
- Each: icon, 2–3 word title, one supporting sentence framed as a benefit (not a raw spec).
- **Priority:** Must-have.

### 4.4 Detail accordions
Modelled on Season Three's collapsed sections + Huru's depth.
- Collapsible sections keep the page clean:
  - **Description** — benefit-led paragraph.
  - **Specifications** — capacity (30 cans / 30L), dimensions, weight, materials, pockets — each spec paired with why it matters.
  - **Shipping** — transparent delivery times, dispatch info, honestly stated (see §6).
  - **Returns & Guarantee** — clear, honest policy (see §6).
  - **Care** — how to clean/maintain.
- **Priority:** Must-have (Description, Specs, Shipping, Returns); Care is nice-to-have.

### 4.5 Lifestyle / storytelling section
- A band of real-use imagery (beach, hike, festival, campsite) with short captions.
- Reinforces the "rather be outside" identity and doubles as ad creative.
- **Priority:** Strong add-on.

### 4.6 Reviews block
Modelled on Huru.
- Star-rating summary + count, distribution bars, and several real, detailed written reviews.
- Photo reviews / UGC where possible.
- **Priority:** Must-have (even a modest number of genuine reviews; never fabricate — see §6).

### 4.7 Cross-sell / "You may also like"
- Simple row of complementary products (ice packs, dry bag, packing cubes) once catalogue supports it.
- **Priority:** Nice-to-have (post-launch).

### 4.8 Email capture
- Inline or exit-intent capture with a small incentive (10% off first order).
- **Priority:** Strong add-on.

### 4.9 Footer
- Clear links: shipping, returns, contact, policies, socials.
- Reinforce trust (secure checkout, returns promise).
- **Priority:** Must-have.

## 5. FAQ page — structure & requirements

### 5.1 Purpose
Handle objections and reduce support tickets before they happen. For a dropshipped product, the FAQ is a key trust surface — especially around delivery times, which are dropshipping's weak point. Answering honestly here builds credibility rather than eroding it.

### 5.2 Structure
Group questions under clear headings so shoppers can scan to their concern:
- **Shipping & Delivery** — realistic delivery times, dispatch info, tracking, where it ships from, international.
- **Returns & Refunds** — the policy (once finalised), how to start a return, timeframes, condition requirements.
- **The Product** — leakproof (confirmed), how long it keeps things cold, capacity (30 cans / 30L), how to clean/care, materials.
- **Ordering & Payment** — accepted payment methods, order changes/cancellations, secure checkout reassurance.

### 5.3 Requirements
- Accordion/expandable format for easy scanning on mobile.
- Honest, specific answers (no vague "ships soon" — give real ranges).
- Tone consistent with brand voice (helpful, effortless).
- Link to Contact page for anything not covered.
- Cross-link from product page accordions and footer.
- **Priority:** Must-have (Shipping, Product); Returns section pending policy decision.

## 6. Contact page — structure & requirements

### 6.1 Purpose
Reassure customers there are real people behind Rove who'll help — a critical trust signal for a new brand.

### 6.2 Structure & content
- **Heading:** "Got a question? We're on it."
- **Intro:** friendly line stating we're real people happy to help with orders or choosing the right product.
- **Contact form:** name, email, order number (optional), message.
- **Support reassurances under the form:**
  - We reply within 24 hours (Mon–Fri).
  - Easy returns *(finalise once policy is set)*.
  - Have your order number ready for order queries.
- **Support channel(s):** email address; social DMs if used.
- Optional mini-FAQ snippet or link to full FAQ.

### 6.3 Requirements
- Warm, on-brand tone — not a bare form.
- Mobile-friendly form; confirmation message on submit.
- Reachable from header/footer nav.
- **Priority:** Must-have.

## 7. Cart page — structure & requirements

### 7.1 Purpose
Protect and grow the sale at the highest-intent moment. Reduce abandonment by reinforcing trust and nudging order value up.

### 7.2 Structure & content
- **Line items:** clear product image, name, colour/variant, quantity editor, price.
- **Free-shipping progress:** e.g. "You're £X away from free UK delivery" (if a threshold is used) — proven to lift order value.
- **Trust reinforcement at checkout moment:** secure-checkout badges, returns/guarantee reminder, honest dispatch note.
- **Single clear primary CTA:** "Checkout" — no competing buttons.
- **Cross-sell / upsell row:** simple complementary items (ice packs, dry bag, packing cubes) to raise average order value.
- **Reassurance microcopy:** e.g. "Tracked delivery · Easy returns · Secure payment."

### 7.3 Requirements
- Mobile-first; frictionless quantity edits and easy return-to-shopping.
- Fast load; no distracting pop-ups that block checkout.
- Accurate GBP totals including any shipping logic.
- **Priority:** Must-have (core cart); cross-sell row is a strong add-on.

## 8. Content requirements

### 8.1 Copy
- Product name + evocative colour names (Season Three approach).
- Benefit-led hero one-liner.
- "Why Rove" benefit blocks.
- Full benefit-led description (supplier "FORICH" branding and broken English removed entirely).
- Specs framed as benefits.
- Honest shipping & returns copy.
- Brand voice: confident, effortless, a little wanderlust — "throw it in the car and go."

### 8.2 Imagery
- **Highest-priority asset.** Consistent, branded imagery is the single biggest conversion lever.
- Requirements: uniform background treatment, consistent aspect ratio (1:1 for grid, plus lifestyle shots), consistent lighting/framing, no supplier text overlays, sensible file names.
- At least one strong lifestyle hero shot; ideally a set of real-use images distinct from the stock supplier photos every competitor uses.
- **Note:** No tool can invent genuine new photos of the physical product — the requirement is to *standardise and restyle* available images and source/create lifestyle context.

## 9. Trust & honesty guardrails (critical)

Both reference brands are *real* companies with genuinely bespoke products; Rove is dropshipping. Borrow their **presentation and structure**, never claims that aren't true.

- **Guarantee:** Offer only what can be honoured. A "lifetime guarantee" that can't be serviced is a liability. Use a realistic promise (e.g. 30-day returns + a 1-year defect policy) and spell it out Huru-style.
- **No fabricated authenticity cues:** Avoid "unique serial number," "our own design," or bespoke-manufacturing implications that aren't real.
- **Reviews must be genuine.** Never fabricate reviews or ratings. Seed with real customer reviews as they come in; use supplier/aggregated reviews only if platform-compliant and clearly legitimate.
- **Shipping transparency:** State realistic delivery times honestly (dropshipping's weak point). Honesty here reduces chargebacks and "where's my order" tickets and builds trust rather than eroding it.
- **Leakproof claim: confirmed** — the product is verified leakproof, so this can be stated with confidence as a headline benefit.
- **Remaining product claims** (hours-cold, capacity) must reflect the actual product — verify with a sample order before publishing.
